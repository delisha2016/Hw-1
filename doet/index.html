Usability Problems in Everyday Objects

Problem 1: washing machine Control Panel

Description:
The control panel on the washing machine dial shown above has small, hard-to-read buttons and an overly complex interface. The labels on the buttons are written in tiny text, making it difficult to select the right function. Additionally, the layout is cluttered with too many options, some of which are rarely used.

Usability Issues:

  • Learnability: It is challenging for new users to understand how to operate the washing machine due to the complex interface. The lack of easy design forces users to constantly refer to the manual.
  • Efficiency: It takes longer than necessary to input commands, especially when setting a timer or adjusting the load and water content in the machine , due to the small, cramped buttons.
  • Safety: The difficulty in reading the labels and understanding the controls could lead to incorrect settings, such as washing with warm water instead of cold which will cause the colors in colored clothes to run

Suggested Fix:
Redesign the control panel to have larger buttons with clear, legible labels. Simplify the interface by grouping rarely used functions under a "More Options" menu, leaving the most common functions easily accessible. An intuitive layout with visual cues could also help in making the oven more user-friendly.

Problem 2: Light Switch Panel

Description:
In my apartment, the light switches are all identical and placed in a row without any labeling, making it difficult to know which switch controls which light. This is especially problematic in areas with multiple light sources, such as the living room.

Usability Issues:

  • Learnability: It’s hard for new users or guests to quickly understand which switch controls which light. There’s no visual indication or label to guide them.
  • Efficiency: Users often have to try multiple switches before finding the correct one, which wastes time.
  • Safety: In the dark, fumbling with switches could lead to potential hazards, especially if there are obstacles in the room.

Suggested Fix:
Label each switch with a small, clear icon or text indicating the light it controls. Alternatively, use different styles or colors of switches for different light sources, making it easier to identify the correct one at a glance.

Problem 3: Door Handle with Keyhole

Description:
The door handle on my front door has the keyhole placed directly beneath the handle. The keyhole is small and not illuminated, making it difficult to insert the key, especially at night.

Usability Issues:

  • Learnability: The keyhole’s placement beneath the handle is not immediately obvious, leading to confusion.
  • Efficiency: It’s difficult to quickly unlock the door, especially in low-light conditions, due to the keyhole’s small size and poor visibility.
  • Safety: Struggling to insert the key at night could delay entry into the house, potentially putting the user in an unsafe situation.

Suggested Fix:
Consider relocating the keyhole to a more visible and accessible position, possibly integrating a small LED light to illuminate the area. Another option could be to use a larger keyhole or a more intuitive locking mechanism, such as a keypad.

Conclusion

In analyzing these everyday objects, it’s clear that usability is often compromised in favor of aesthetics or cost-saving measures. By focusing on the LES dimensions—Learnability, Efficiency, and Safety—designers can create more user-friendly products that are both functional and intuitive.
